# AI Council Governance

*MMA 616 — Governed Claude in the Classroom*

> **Rule of the project:** Nothing is deployed, presented as reliable, or used in a classroom workflow until the AI Council has reviewed it.

The AI Council is the **human governance layer** of this project. This document defines what the Council is, why it exists, what it reviews, how it decides, and how its work is documented and presented.

---

## Purpose of the AI Council

The AI Council reviews all information, outputs, assumptions, risks, and evaluation results before the dashboard or any Claude-supported workflow is used with real users. Its job is to confirm the system is accurate, appropriate, ethical, and aligned with the classroom use case **before** anything is released.

The Council is **not** there to slow the project down. It is there to make sure the system is trustworthy before it touches real students or instructors — and to keep accountability with humans, not with the model.

## Why governance is required

Claude can produce fluent, confident output that is wrong, biased, or out of scope. In a classroom, a bad output is not harmless — it can mislead a student, misrepresent an instructor's expectations, or influence how work is assessed. Governance is the mechanism that separates **AI capability** from **human judgment**: Claude generates claims; the Council decides whether those claims are good enough to act on. Without this layer, the project would be a demo, not a governed intelligence system.

## What the AI Council reviews

The Council reviews:

- The project objective and classroom use case.
- The primary user and the decision being supported.
- The data sources or course materials used.
- The `CLAUDE.md` specification.
- The dashboard views and outputs.
- Claude-generated summaries, recommendations, and classifications.
- Known-answer test cases and evaluation results.
- Risks related to accuracy, bias, privacy, student interpretation, and overreliance.
- Whether the system is ready for deployment, needs revision, or should remain a prototype.

## Approval criteria

The Council approves the project for deployment only when the system is judged to be:

1. **Accurate** — output matches the source material, rubric, or data.
2. **Useful** — output supports a real classroom decision or workflow.
3. **Clear** — users can understand what the dashboard shows and why.
4. **Appropriate** — the system does not overstep the role of instructor, student, or teaching team.
5. **Governed** — human review points are clearly defined.
6. **Safe to use** — no exposure of sensitive information and no misleading conclusions.
7. **Aligned with scope** — the tool does only what the project says it does.
8. **Evidence-based** — Claude's claims are grounded in approved sources (course material, rubric, dataset).

## Deployment rule

> **Deployment is not allowed until the AI Council approves the system, or approves it with revisions.**

A Council review ends in exactly one of three outcomes:

- **Approve** — ready for use in the defined classroom scenario.
- **Approve with revisions** — usable once named changes are made; the Council records the required changes.
- **Do not deploy** — the system stays in prototype use until it is re-reviewed.

"Deployment" in this classroom context means using the tool in the realistic classroom scenario described in the **Use** stage — with real users and real classroom decisions — not a private team test.

## AI Council Review Packet — checklist

Before any deployment decision, the team prepares an **AI Council Review Packet** containing:

- [ ] **Project summary** — what the system is and the framing as a governed classroom intelligence system.
- [ ] **User and decision supported** — primary user and the decision/workflow.
- [ ] **Data sources used** — the approved course material, rubric, or dataset.
- [ ] **Dashboard / app feature summary** — screenshots or a written summary of each view.
- [ ] **Key outputs generated by Claude** — representative summaries, recommendations, or classifications.
- [ ] **Known-answer test cases** — the 5–10 cases used for evaluation.
- [ ] **Baseline evaluation results** — scores and notes before revision.
- [ ] **Revised evaluation results** — scores and notes after improvement.
- [ ] **Known risks** — accuracy, bias, privacy, student interpretation, overreliance.
- [ ] **Human review points** — where a human must check or override.
- [ ] **Recommended deployment status** — the team's recommendation.
- [ ] **Final recommendation** — approve / approve with revisions / do not deploy.

## Human accountability rules

These rules keep accountability with people, not the model:

- **Claude outputs are claims, not facts.**
- **Human users remain accountable** for every final decision.
- **The AI Council must review outputs before deployment.**
- Claude must **cite or reference** the course material, rubric, or data behind its output.
- Claude must **not invent** course policies, grades, or instructor expectations.
- Claude must **flag uncertainty**.
- Claude must **identify when a human** (student, instructor, TA, or Council member) should review something manually.
- Claude must **not replace instructor judgment**.
- **Sensitive student information is minimized or anonymized** if used.
- **Any student-facing output is reviewed** before use.
- **Anything that could affect grades, assessment, or student perception requires human approval.**

## How to present the governance story

In the final presentation, the governance layer should be told as a strength, not a disclaimer. Cover:

- **Why governance is necessary** here specifically (a wrong classroom output has real consequences).
- **How the AI Council review process works** — what it reviews and the approval criteria.
- **What the Council flagged** in the baseline review.
- **What changed** after the Council's review.
- **The final decision** — approved, approved with revisions, or not approved — supported by the Decision Log.

Use the completed **Review Packet** and **Decision Log** as appendix evidence that the governance process actually ran — not just that it was described.
